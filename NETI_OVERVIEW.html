The teachings of Shri Ranjit Maharaj — preserved, searchable, and citable
An overview for the humans of the project · July 2026

1What NETI is

For thirty years, the talks of Ranjit Maharaj were recorded on tapes, typed by devoted hands, scanned page by page, and collected into an archive of more than fifty thousand files. NETI turns that archive into something a scholar can ask questions of — without ever altering a single original.

The test NETI must pass is simple to say: for any concept — “the role of the guru”, “surrender”, “the four bodies” — produce a complete, cited, deduplicated dossier of what the Master said about it. Every quote traceable to its exact page or tape position. Nothing invented, nothing paraphrased, nothing lost.

2The four layers — how nothing is ever lost

Everything in NETI lives in one of four layers. The rule that makes the whole system safe: each layer can only be built from the one above it, and the top layer is untouchable.

Sources

The originals — sacred and sealed The scans, tapes, and videos exactly as they were given to us. Nothing here is ever moved, renamed, edited, or deleted. The system is physically unable to write here. New material can arrive; nothing can leave or change.

▼ machines read, never touch
Canonical

The readable text — machine-made, remakeable Every scanned page, read by text-recognition software and cut into the natural units of a satsang: the question, the Master’s answer, the translator’s relay. If we ever improve the software, we simply regenerate this layer overnight. It is never edited by hand.

▼ humans judge, machines remember
Curation

Human scholarship — the precious layer Every decision a person makes — “this correction is right”, “this passage belongs in the guru dossier”, “this speaker is Maharaj” — is recorded here with a name, a date, and a reason. This layer survives everything. It is the project’s real treasure, because the scarcest resource is not computer time: it is your hours of judgment.

▼ rebuilt at will
Derived

The search machinery — completely disposable Indexes, search databases, reading views. If any of it breaks or a better technology appears, we delete it and rebuild it from the layers above with one command. Nothing irreplaceable lives here — by design.

This is why the corrections dilemma never bites us: when a machine re-reads a page better than before, your past corrections and judgments are not overwritten — they are re-checked against the new text, and anything that no longer matches is flagged for your eyes, never silently discarded.

3The pipeline — from a scanned page to a searchable answer

InventoryWalk every file, fingerprint it, classify it. Duplicates found by mathematics, not guesswork.
AssembleGroup 54,555 files into ~4,800 logical documents — one talk may be eighty page-scans plus a PDF plus a tape.
ExtractRead each page (rotating it first if it was scanned sideways), split the text into question-and-answer turns.
VerifyEvery single page gets a verdict: readable English, or an honest flag. Gibberish cannot enter — the system refuses it.
EnrichHarvest thirty years of human indexing — dates, topics, tables of contents — into proposals for a person to confirm.
SearchBuild the indexes that answer questions — by exact words today, by meaning next.

Every stage can be re-run safely at any time; every stage leaves an evidence report; no stage ever writes into the sources.

The quality story, in one anecdote

Three talks from February 1998 were scanned sideways decades ago, and no one could read what the software made of them — pages of alphabet soup. The system’s own census found the problem, the fix was tested on those exact pages before touching anything, every one of the corpus’s 25,329 pages was then re-verified individually — and three lost Mumbai talks came back to the archive, word by word. That is the working standard: no result is trusted until the evidence is on the table.

4Concept dossiers — the reason it all exists

A dossier is a living answer to one research question. It is how the archive becomes scholarship — and eventually, how translations get their foundation.

  1. A question is asked. “What did the Master say about the role of the guru?” — typed by you, or seeded automatically from the topic indexes Sachin hand-made for every month of talks.
  2. The machine hunts. It rephrases the question many ways, searches the whole corpus, reads the candidates, and gathers passages — each one mechanically verified, letter-for-letter, against the canonical text. A misquoted or “improved” quotation is structurally impossible.
  3. A human judges. You keep, discard, annotate, and group. This is the only work that requires a person — and the system is built so it is the only work a person does. No commands, no file wrangling, no syntax. Judgment only.
  4. The dossier renders itself. A readable document — every passage cited to its page or tape second — regenerated on every change. When new material enters the archive, the question re-runs itself and shows you only what is new.
John’s method for translating the Dasbodh — first study everything the masters said about a concept, then translate with that understanding — is the method NETI industrialises. The dossier is that study, made cheap, durable, and findable.

5The working principles — in plain words

The Master’s voice is the source

His broken, forceful, hammering English is never “improved”, normalised, or modernised — not by software, not by AI, not by us. Even his transcriber’s reverent capitals (“you are HE”) are protected by explicit rules.

Machines propose, humans decide

Every automated suggestion — a speaker’s identity, a topic, a duplicate, a correction — is a candidate until a named person confirms it. The machine is never the judge of record.

Evidence over assertion

“It works” is not a sentence this project accepts. Every claim traces to a report from a real run; every review re-derives the numbers independently before believing them.

Everything signed, everything reversible

Every human decision carries a name, a date, and a reason — forever. Every machine layer can be deleted and rebuilt. The combination means mistakes are cheap and scholarship is permanent.
