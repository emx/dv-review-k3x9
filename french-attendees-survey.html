The teaching that wouldn't land

What teaching of Shri Ranjit Maharaj is poorly understood by his French attendees? Read across the Brittany and Paris talks, the answer is consistent — and he addresses the misunderstanding himself, repeatedly.

“Everything is illusion” — and its corollary, that there is therefore nothing to do, only understand — is the teaching the French audiences persistently fail to absorb. The misunderstanding resurfaces in two characteristic forms across five seasons of talks: resisting “illusion” as if it were a claim about physics, and converting the no-method teaching back into a method. A translation gap that Maharaj himself names compounds both.

Form 1

Taking “illusion” as a claim about physics — and resisting it

The questioners hear “the world is illusion” as a statement that contradicts the evidence of their senses, and push back with objects, values, and purposes.

  • The carpet objection

    Question

    I would like to ask You about the illusion. When You say everything is illusion, I have trouble. I can accept it, what You are saying. But to me, for example, this is a carpet. And it exists as a carpet.

    Maharaj dissolves the carpet step by step — wool, earth, ashes, water, H₂O, wind, space — to show “Illusion means which is not. You see that, but still, it is not.” And the counterweight often missed: “But still, you should not destroy it, no need to destroy.”

    Brittany, 19 Jun 1997 · pp. 1–2 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-19-brittany#x0001

  • After a full week: “but what about love?”

    Question

    She said, all week You talked of the illusion, what is illusion, the world is illusion. But You didn’t say anything about the aspect of Reality, which is love, according to her.

    Seven days of talks, and the point still hasn't landed — the clearest single piece of evidence in the corpus. His answer explains the pedagogy itself: “There is always nothing to say about the Reality, because It is there… Everybody is in illusion, so we have to talk more for illusion. If illusion goes off, what remains.”

    Paris, 3 Jun 2000 · pp. 1–12 · ranjit/english-lectures/2000-06-03-paris#x0001

  • Giving illusion a purpose

    A questioner tries to make illusion instrumental — “can it have a purpose to bring us back to Reality?” — a category error under the teaching (which is not, is not for anything), and the exchange stalls: “But why you say again illusion?… She says in a different way. And I cannot understand her, that's the difficulty.”

    Brittany, 21 Jun 1997 · pp. 34–35 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-21-brittany#x0011

  • Mind, feeling, or another way?

    Still circling the same point in 2000: “this understanding that the world is illusion, does this understanding come through the mind, or we feel it, or is there another way?”

    Paris, 6 Jun 2000 · ranjit/english-lectures/2000-06-06-paris#x0001

Form 2

Converting the no-method teaching back into a method

The teaching is that understanding alone suffices — there is nothing to practice. The French questioners repeatedly ask for something to practice, which inverts the point.

  • Meditating on the void

    Two consecutive days in 1996: “Is to meditate on the void, on the nothingness, a good approach to Reality, or is it not?” — and the next day, concentrating on “the space between two thoughts.” His rebuke names the failure mode exactly:

    Maharaj

    What do you say, what you mean to say, at least say in simple words, don’t use big big words, ‘void’, and all these things. What do you feel in your mind?

    You people hear the words and then you use the words, but don’t know. You hear from me and you use the words now, that ‘void’ and all these things.

    Brittany, 12–13 Jun 1996 · pp. 24–26 · ranjit/english-lectures/1996-06-12-brittany#x0001 · 1996-06-13-brittany#x0008

  • Asking for the practice

    The same request keeps returning across the seasons: “Is it necessary to practise that in order to understand Reality?” (1997) · “should we concentrate totally on Reality… Or do we have something to do in the world?” (1998) · “Till what point we should concentrate on Reality? And when should we surrender?” (1998).

    Brittany · 1997-06-28-brittany#x0004 · 1998-06-03-brittany#x0020 · 1998-06-04-brittany#x0002

  • The contradiction question

    By 2000 the practice-seeking has sharpened into paradox-hunting: “To reach Reality, do we have to use the mind? And if yes… isn’t it a contradiction to use the mind to reach Reality?” — the question his Master's vihangam maarga (realization by thinking) answers, but which keeps being asked as a trap rather than understood as the way.

    Brittany, 17 Jun 2000 · ranjit/english-lectures/2000-06-17-brittany#x0001

Compounding factor

The language gap — named by Maharaj himself

  • Through the translator, twice removed

    “You don't follow. She doesn't follow. I could not understand your language, no, that's the difficulty to understand. And she translates, but she also cannot understand, what to do?”

    Brittany, 28 Jun 1997 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-28-brittany#x0015

  • The French Master Key

    Comparing the English Master Key to Self Realization with its French translation: “When they make the translation, something always happens. Because the writer, his mind, is not the mind of the translator.”

    Brittany, 28 Jun 1997 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-28-brittany#x0003

  • Mid-answer interruptions

    The Brittany transcripts show answers cut off by translation — e.g. the 1996 exchange where his introduction of Ramdas's Dasbodh (“He says at one place...”) is interrupted by the translator relaying a different question, and the thread is lost.

    Brittany, 7 Jun 1996 · p. 12 · ranjit/english-lectures/1996-06-07-brittany#x0023

Method

How this was established — and its limits

Sources. Lexical and semantic passes over the corpus: explicit mentions of French; semantic searches for incomprehension (“people do not understand what I say”), translation loss, practice-seeking (“what should I practice, give me a method”), and illusion-resistance (“you say everything is illusion but the world feels real”); ~10 French-venue exchanges read in full.

Caveat. This is a synthesis, not a claim Maharaj makes explicitly — the corpus never has him say “the French don't understand X.” American audiences raise the illusion objection too (Sedona: “My mind is screaming at the idea that the mountains… are all illusion”; San Diego 1999). What distinguishes the French corpus is the practice-seeking variant — void-meditation, concentration schedules, surrender timing — and the week-long persistence of the illusion question in Paris, June 2000. Italic fragments throughout are verbatim effective text; everything else is editorial summary.
