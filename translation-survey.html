NETI corpus · thematic survey

“Translated in words only”

Everything the corpus holds on translation — Shri Ranjit Maharaj taught foreigners through interpreters, from translated books, in his second language; here is what he said about the act itself, in three movements: the doctrine, the books, and the translator sitting beside him.

Method. Lexical passes on translation and translator translated (50 ranked hits) plus a semantic pass; most hits are stage directions — the substantive exchanges below were read in full. Quotation panels are verbatim effective text; italic fragments in running text likewise.

Movement 1

The doctrine: translation cannot carry the thing itself

  • “By translating, you can't know”

    Speaking of a man who had made his living translating the books of the Saints:

    Maharaj

    Translating. He was a translator. By translating, you can't know.

    Mumbai, 30 Jan 1997 · p. 30 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-01-30#x0028

  • “They have translated in words only”

    A questioner observes how difficult it is to translate between languages; the answer is about every translator of the Saints:

    Maharaj

    His heart, they didn't know. One who has translated, he doesn't know His heart, no, what He wants to say. They have translated in words only.

    Disciple

    ... ... it doesn't give the meaning.

    Maharaj

    Meaning, yes. So, it is told, no, half knowledge is dangerous. Half knowledge is dangerous. Have the full knowledge, complete, and then do it, it's okay.

    19 Dec 1996 · pp. 7–8 · ranjit/english-lectures/1996-12-19-lecture1#x0007

  • “Translation loses a lot of substance” — “Exactly!”

    The context is the Gujarati translator of the Dasbodh, whose preface dismissed everything after the 10th chapter as written “haphazardly” — precisely the chapters Maharaj holds highest:

    Question

    Translation loses a lot of substance.

    Maharaj

    Exactly! But translator, he also said in a different way. Translation may be wrong, I don't say nay. He may not be of that category. […]

    Translate it, but translation, at least something should be there, no. Because he didn't know. So, reading of books also, so much trouble is there. Always differs. Every mind differs.

    So better to read with the help of the Master. Then you can understand.

    Houston, 3 Jun 1997 · pp. 46–47 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-03-houston#x0011

Movement 2

Translated books, in his hands

He handled translations constantly — checking them, endorsing them with reservations, and honoring the rare ones made by translators who knew.

  • The French Master Key, caught diverging

    Mid-session in Brittany, comparing Siddharameshwar's book in English against its French translation:

    Maharaj

    Translation, something wrong in the translation. I wanted to check. There is some mistake in writing. This differs always. You follow me?

    It's alright, nothing is wrong, but it's here quite different (in English Master Key book), and quite different is here (in French translation). When they make the translation, something always happens. Because the writer, his mind, is not the mind of the trans…

    Brittany, 28 Jun 1997 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-28-brittany#x0003 (OCR cuts the final word)

  • The English Dasbodh, endorsed with a shrug

    “It is in English also available. But the meaning may be, I don't know, what meaning he has done. If he has translated only, okay. So, read that, no harm. When I give understanding, and with that understanding if you read, you can understand the inner meaning also.” Same stance a month earlier: “Haan, Ramdas book is okay, do read… That is also in English it is there.”

    Sedona, 8 May 1998 · pp. 30–33 · 1998-05-08am-sedona#x0022 · 6 Mar 1998 · pp. 64–65 · 1998-03-06#x0031

  • Checking a translation live

    “One stanza has been explained in Marathi. Now they have translated into English, I don't know what is written. I wanted to read.”

    7 Aug 1998 · p. 87 · ranjit/english-lectures/1998-08-07#x0023

  • The translations he honors

    The judgment is not that translation is worthless — it is that only a translator who knows can translate the heart and not just the words. Three he praises in one Houston answer: the anonymous Marathi translator of Shankaracharya's Sadachar — “he has translated so nicely, you can understand the real meaning of that 54 stanzas. He has not given his name also. Up to now nobody knows” — Ranganath Swami's Yoga Vasistha, and Saint Jnaneshwar, “who has translated Bhagwad Gita into Marathi.”

    Houston, 3 Jun 1997 · pp. 1–23 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-06-03-houston#x0002

Movement 3

The translator in the room

Every European talk ran through an interpreter — and the transcripts record what that cost, and how lightly he carried it.

  • The translator's confession

    At Krahberg, the German translator admits that the teaching itself is interfering with his translating:

    Translator

    I just said that, while I listen to You, I begin to rethink somethings. So, you know, translation isn’t an easy thing, because I also may have had other thoughts about something and some teachings. You understand?

    So, I may have some difficulties because of just the fact that You are opening, you know, some ... thoughts in me.

    Maharaj

    If you don’t understand something, and some thoughts may be there, so you can forget something, and say anything else, what harm is there! […] You had some thoughts in your mind, so you cannot explain, give my words to them. Nothing more, no harm. Why should I worry. […]

    So, nothing, no harm about, don’t worry. What is there. They are in illusion, so illusion will be more in them. And that can be rectified afterwards.

    Krahberg, 6 May 1997 · pp. 44–48 · ranjit/english-lectures/1997-05-06-krahberg#x0029

  • Twice removed

    The chain questioner → translator → Maharaj, breaking in both directions: “You don't follow. She doesn't follow. I could not understand your language, no, that's the difficulty to understand. And she translates, but she also cannot understand, what to do?” (Brittany) · “I don't know French language. What you say, I can't understand. Ask him again.” (Paris) · “Without translation it's very difficult ... I can't follow his words, what to do?” (Mumbai)

    1997-06-28-brittany#x0015 · 1998-05-20-paris#x0017 · 1998-08-19#x0011

Coda

A corpus footnote

There is a structural echo worth noticing: a whole document kind in NETI is translation — the English renderings of Maharaj's Marathi pravachans (the Dasbodh talks, Manache Shlok, and the rest). The corpus itself does the thing he kept warning about.

Which is presumably why its ground rule exists: the Master's voice is served as effective text — canonical plus curated corrections — and never editorially “improved.” His own resolution of the tension is the Sedona line: the translation is fine, provided the understanding comes from elsewhere. The words are the address, not the place.
